Nikola Tesla

A visionary Serbian-American inventor and engineer whose groundbreaking work in electricity and magnetism revolutionized the modern world.

A timeline of Nikola Tesla's Life

    Early Life and Education
  • 1856: Born on July 10th in the village of Smiljan, in the Austrian Empire (present-day Croatia), to a Serbian Orthodox priest and a mother who invented household appliances.
  • 1875: Enrolled at the Austrian Polytechnic in Graz, where he excelled in mathematics and physics but struggled with certain subjects, particularly those related to practical applications.
  • 1879: Left school due to illness and a disagreement with a professor.
  • 1881: Worked as an electrical engineer in Budapest, where he conceived the idea for the induction motor, a revolutionary device that would later power the world.
    Early Career and Innovations
  • 1882: Developed detailed plans for the induction motor, which would operate on alternating current (AC) power.
  • 1884: Immigrated to the United States, seeking opportunities to further his work.
  • 1885: Worked for Thomas Edison's company, but their differing views on direct current (DC) and AC power led to a professional rift.
  • 1886: Founded the Tesla Electric Light Company, aiming to develop and promote AC power systems.
  • 1888: Delivered a groundbreaking lecture at the American Institute of Electrical Engineers, outlining the principles of AC power and its advantages over DC power.
      • The "War of Currents" and AC Power Triumph
  • Late 1880s: Engaged in a famous rivalry with Thomas Edison, known as the "War of Currents," as they competed to establish their respective power systems.
  • 1893: Won the contract to illuminate the Chicago World's Fair with AC power, a significant victory that demonstrated the superiority of his system.
  • 1895: Built a high-voltage, high-frequency laboratory in New York City, where he conducted experiments with X-rays, wireless communication, and other cutting-edge technologies.
  • 1896: Demonstrated wireless communication by transmitting radio signals over a distance of several miles.
      • Later Years and Controversial Ideas
  • Late 1890s: Pioneered wireless power transmission, envisioning a world where energy could be transmitted wirelessly over long distances.
  • Early 1900s: Embarked on the ambitious Wardenclyffe Tower project, aiming to transmit wireless power and messages around the world. However, due to financial constraints and technical challenges, the project was ultimately abandoned.
  • 1917: Received the Edison Medal, a prestigious award recognizing his significant contributions to electrical engineering, despite the historical rivalry between the two inventors.
  • 1943: Died in New York City on January 7th, leaving behind a legacy of innovation and a mind far ahead of its time.
